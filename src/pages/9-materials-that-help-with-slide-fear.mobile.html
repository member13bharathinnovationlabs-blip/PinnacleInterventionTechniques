What's Happening in Your Child's Brain
The Vestibular System
Three tiny fluid-filled canals in the inner ear detect movement, gravity, and head position. It's the body's internal gyroscope — telling the brain exactly where the body is in space at every moment.
In typical development, this system perceives movement as neutral or pleasant. A slide feels fun. A swing feels exciting.
When the System Over-Responds
In your child, this system is hypersensitive. It over-responds to movement input, perceiving ordinary movement as extreme or dangerous. A small slide feels like a cliff drop. Slight tilting feels like falling into a void.
This vestibular over-response triggers the amygdala — the brain's alarm center — activating a full fight-flight-freeze response. Stress hormones flood the system. Heart rate spikes. Muscles lock.
This is a wiring difference, not a behavior problem.

ACT II — THE CLINICAL FRAMEWORK
What Is Graduated Vestibular Desensitization?
Graduated vestibular desensitization is a structured, stepwise therapeutic approach that gently and systematically exposes a child's nervous system to movement experiences it currently finds threatening — beginning at a level the child can tolerate, and incrementally building from there.
The key word is graduated. Not forced. Not rushed. Not "just push them through it." The nervous system learns safety through repeated, successful, non-threatening experiences with movement. Each successful exposure rewires the amygdala's threat response, gradually recalibrating what the vestibular system perceives as dangerous.
Child-Led Pacing
The child sets the pace. Therapist and caregiver follow the child's nervous system signals.
Incremental Exposure
Each step is only slightly more challenging than the last — staying within the child's tolerance window.
Positive Association
Movement is paired with safety, play, and caregiver presence — not pressure or performance.
The Gravitational Insecurity Spectrum
Not all slide fear looks the same. Gravitational insecurity exists on a spectrum, and understanding where your child falls helps you calibrate the intervention correctly. Recognizing the presentation guides which materials and steps will be most effective for your child's unique nervous system profile.
1
Mild
Hesitates briefly, needs one adult prompt, will attempt slide with encouragement
2
Moderate
Refuses repeatedly, physical stiffening when approached, will attempt with sustained support
3
Significant
Meltdown on approach, avoids playground entirely, body-level panic response
4
Severe
Generalized movement avoidance beyond slides — swings, ramps, stairs, being lifted

Clinical Note: Children presenting at the "significant" or "severe" level should be formally evaluated by a licensed Occupational Therapist trained in Sensory Integration before beginning a home protocol. This page supports mild-to-moderate presentations and OT-guided home carryover.
Signs Your Child May Have Gravitational Insecurity
Gravitational insecurity often shows up in everyday moments long before the playground becomes a challenge. These signs — when clustered together — are meaningful signals that your child's vestibular system may need therapeutic support. You may recognize several of these from your child's daily life.
Behavioral Signs
  • Refuses slides, swings, or climbing structures
  • Clings to caregiver when feet leave ground
  • Panics when tilted back (e.g., hair washing)
  • Resists being picked up or carried
  • Freezes at the top of stairs
  • Won't jump, even from low heights
Physical Signs
  • White-knuckle gripping on any elevated surface
  • Rigid body posture when movement is anticipated
  • Wide eyes, rapid breathing, elevated heart rate
  • Vomiting or nausea with mild movement
  • Hypervigilance on uneven surfaces
  • Toe-walking or unusual gait patterns
The 5-Phase Desensitization Framework
Every effective graduated vestibular desensitization protocol follows a five-phase progression. This framework is the backbone of the 9-material protocol presented on this page. Do not skip phases — each one prepares the nervous system for the next.
Each phase may take days, weeks, or months depending on the child's nervous system. Progress is nonlinear — regression is normal and expected. Celebrate every step forward, no matter how small it appears to outside observers.

Material 1 of 9
Platform Swing (Frog Swing / Bolster Swing)
What It Is
A wide, flat, low-to-ground suspended platform that provides slow, linear vestibular input — the gentlest form of movement-based sensory experience available in therapy equipment.
Why It Works
Linear, predictable movement in a seated, supported position introduces vestibular input at the lowest threat level. The child controls how much movement happens. The wide surface eliminates fear of falling. The brain begins to associate movement with safety rather than danger.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Place child on swing while it is completely still. Let them sit for as long as needed.
  1. With verbal consent ("Can I give it a tiny push?"), introduce the smallest possible linear sway — less than 2 inches.
  1. Narrate: "I've got you. See how slow and smooth that is?"
  1. Gradually increase arc only as child signals comfort (relaxed grip, eye contact, vocalization).
  1. Session length: 5–10 minutes maximum. Never force continuation.
DIY Alternative
Blanket hammock suspended between two adults at hip height. Child sits or lies inside while adults create gentle, slow, linear sway.
Age Adaptation
Ages 2–3: Parent holds child in lap on swing. Ages 4–6: Child sits independently with parent standing nearby. Ages 7–8: Child may hold ropes independently.

Safety Note: Always ensure swing hardware is rated for combined adult + child weight if parent rides with child. Maximum session: 10 minutes. Stop immediately if child shows nausea, pallor, or distress.
Material 2 of 9
Foam Wedge / Incline Mat
What It Is
A firm foam wedge (typically 6–12 inches high at the peak) that creates a gentle incline. Used to simulate the gravitational challenge of a slide's angle — at floor level, with zero height risk.
Why It Works
The foam wedge deconstructs the slide experience into its simplest component: body orientation on an incline. Without height, without speed, and without the visual overwhelm of a playground, the child's nervous system can experience and process the gravitational tilt in a completely safe context. This is Phase 2–3 work — passive to active ground-level movement.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Begin with child sitting at the base of the wedge facing uphill. Just sitting — no movement required.
  1. Progress to child leaning back against the incline (reclining position). Narrate body position calmly.
  1. Progress to child sliding down the wedge from sitting position — only 6–8 inches of travel.
  1. Add a small sensory reward at the bottom (favorite toy, high five, clapping).
  1. Gradually increase wedge height as tolerance builds.
DIY Alternative
Firm couch cushions stacked at one end under a fitted sheet to create a gentle incline. Ensure stability before use.
Pro Tip
Place a favorite character sticker or small toy at the bottom of the wedge to create a motivating "destination." The brain learns: movement leads to good things.
Material 3 of 9
Therapy Ball (Physioball / Vestibular Ball)
What It Is
A large inflated exercise/therapy ball (45–75 cm diameter depending on child's height) that provides multidirectional, dynamic vestibular input in a controlled, parent-assisted format.
Why It Works
Sitting on a therapy ball requires constant, small adjustments from the vestibular and proprioceptive systems. These micro-adjustments train the nervous system to process movement signals without triggering alarm. The ball moves in all directions — front-back, side-side, and diagonal — giving the vestibular system broad, low-intensity practice. Parent hand placement at hips provides deep proprioceptive input that reduces fear simultaneously.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Child sits on ball with feet flat on floor, parent kneeling behind with hands on hips.
  1. Begin with ball completely still. Hold for 30–60 seconds. Breathe together.
  1. Parent creates very gentle front-to-back sway (1–2 inches). Narrate: "Feel how your body adjusts? Your body is so smart."
  1. Progress to side-to-side movement, then gentle diagonal tilts.
  1. Advanced: Child sits independently and bounces gently — 10–20 slow bounces.
DIY Alternative
Any large inflatable ball from a sporting goods store. Ensure it is firm enough to hold the child's weight without excessive sink.
Age Adaptation
Ages 2–3: Parent holds child securely on ball at all times. Ages 4–5: Child sits independently with parent hands lightly at hips. Ages 6–8: Child bounces or rocks independently.
Material 4 of 9
Body Sock / Compression Suit
What It Is
A full-body stretchy lycra or spandex tube that encases the child, providing constant, gentle compression (deep pressure) to the entire body. Available in multiple sizes from toddler to adult.
Why It Works
Deep pressure input from the body sock activates the proprioceptive system, which has a direct, powerful calming effect on the vestibular system. When the nervous system is pre-loaded with proprioceptive input, it is significantly less reactive to vestibular (movement) input. This is why squeezing a stressed child — or having them do "heavy work" — reduces movement anxiety. The body sock creates a full-body proprioceptive envelope that reduces the amygdala's threat-detection sensitivity before movement activities begin.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Use body sock as a pre-activity calming tool — 5–10 minutes before any vestibular work.
  1. Allow child to climb in independently (or assist gently). Never force entry.
  1. Child can sit, lie, roll, or move inside the sock in any way they choose.
  1. While inside, therapist or parent may apply additional gentle compression to shoulders or hips.
  1. Transition directly from body sock to the vestibular activity while the nervous system is calm.
DIY Alternative
Tight-fitting compression shirt + leggings, or swaddling with a firm blanket (for children who tolerate this). A lycra hammock can also provide similar input.
Safety Note
Ensure there is always an opening the child can exit from independently. Never use body sock as a restraint. Monitor for signs of distress at all times.
Material 5 of 9
Ramp Board / Slide Board (Floor Level)
What It Is
A smooth wooden or plastic board (approximately 4–6 feet long) positioned at a very low angle (10–20 degrees) on the floor — essentially a mini-slide at ground level with no elevation risk whatsoever.
Why It Works
The ramp board is the closest functional analog to an actual playground slide that can be delivered at zero height. It reproduces the core vestibular experience of a slide — body moving down an incline, slight acceleration, gravitational pull forward — without the visual overwhelm of height. This is the critical bridge between floor-level vestibular work and actual playground engagement. Many children who complete ramp board practice are ready to attempt the smallest actual playground slide within 2–4 weeks.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Set ramp at the shallowest possible angle (10 degrees). Child sits at the top with parent hands on shoulders.
  1. First pass: Parent gently guides child down while maintaining full contact. Speed: as slow as possible.
  1. Progress to child initiating their own movement down the ramp with parent hands lightly present.
  1. Increase angle by 5 degrees every 3–5 successful sessions.
  1. Add slight speed only after child initiates movement spontaneously and smiles at the bottom.
DIY Alternative
A smooth, firm piece of plywood (sanded and smoothed) resting on the bottom stair step or a low, stable box. Ensure there are no splinters or sharp edges. Place a non-slip mat at the bottom.
Pro Tip
Place a beloved stuffed animal or toy at the bottom of the ramp. The child "slides to" their toy. The approach destination reduces anticipatory anxiety significantly.
Material 6 of 9
Weighted Lap Pad / Weighted Vest
What It Is
A vest or lap pad filled with small weights (typically 5–10% of the child's body weight) that provides sustained deep pressure and proprioceptive input throughout an activity.
Why It Works
Like the body sock, weighted equipment works by flooding the proprioceptive system with organizing input. Proprioception and vestibular processing are deeply interconnected — when one system is well-regulated, it buffers the other. Wearing a weighted vest during playground visits or vestibular activities gives the nervous system a constant anchor of organized sensory input, reducing the reactivity of the vestibular system to movement challenges. Multiple RCTs confirm weighted vest use reduces anxiety-related behaviors in children with sensory sensitivities.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Introduce weighted vest during calm, preferred activities first (not during vestibular challenges).
  1. Use for 20–minute sessions maximum. Remove and reassess. Never use for more than 2 hours continuously.
  1. Wear vest during playground visits — put it on before entering the playground environment.
  1. Monitor child's response: look for increased calm, reduced stiffening, more willingness to explore.
  1. Consult your child's OT for precise weight calibration (5–10% of body weight is the standard range).
DIY Alternative
A small backpack with a water bottle or light books inside (calibrated to 5% of body weight) worn on the front of the body. A firm, folded blanket placed across the lap during seated activities.
Age Adaptation
Ages 2–3: Weighted lap pad during sitting activities. Ages 4–8: Weighted vest during movement activities and playground visits.
Material 7 of 9
Sensory Tunnel / Crawl Tube
What It Is
A collapsible fabric or plastic tunnel (3–6 feet long, 20–24 inches diameter) that children crawl through on hands and knees, providing both proprioceptive and mild vestibular input through the movement pattern.
Why It Works
Crawling through a tunnel activates the vestibular system through head position changes and forward linear movement — the same movement components present in slide-going — while keeping the child completely in control of their speed, enclosed within a snug, visually defined space. The tactile input from tunnel walls provides simultaneous proprioceptive grounding. Tunnels are also highly motivating for most children, making them an excellent bridge activity that builds movement confidence without the threat of height or speed.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Lay tunnel flat and horizontal first. Let child explore it statically — look inside, touch walls, no movement required.
  1. Place motivating toy at far end. Allow child to retrieve at their own pace.
  1. Progress to timed "races" through the tunnel with a sibling or parent.
  1. Elevate one end slightly (2–3 inches) to introduce downhill crawling — the closest vestibular analog to a slide at this stage.
  1. Elevate further over multiple sessions as confidence builds.
DIY Alternative
A series of dining chairs placed side by side with a large blanket draped over them to form a covered passage. Card table with sheet draped over sides also works well.
Pro Tip
Use a sensory tunnel as a "calm-down station" during playground visits — a place the child can retreat to for proprioceptive regulation before attempting the slide again. It reduces the all-or-nothing quality of playground anxiety.
Material 8 of 9
Visual Schedule / Slide Story Book
What It Is
A custom visual schedule or illustrated "slide story" — a series of pictures or drawings showing the child going through each step of the slide experience, from approaching the playground all the way to celebrating at the bottom.
Why It Works
Anxiety is significantly amplified by unpredictability. The amygdala hyperactivates when it cannot anticipate what comes next. Visual schedules and social stories preload the prefrontal cortex with a predictable narrative sequence, reducing amygdala reactivity before the experience even begins. Research on social stories for anxiety in pediatric populations (Gray, 1994; updated 2015) demonstrates consistent reduction in approach anxiety when children have previewed the experience in illustrated form. For vestibular-anxious children, this cognitive preview is not a bypass of the sensory work — it is an essential complement to it.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Create or print a 6–8 step visual schedule showing: arriving at playground → seeing the slide → climbing the ladder → sitting at the top → feeling nervous (normalize it) → sliding down → landing safely → celebrating.
  1. Read the slide story together at home 1–2 times before each playground visit.
  1. Bring the physical schedule to the playground. Review it together before approaching the slide.
  1. Use the schedule as a reference during the attempt: "We're on step 3 now — you're doing it!"
  1. After completion, mark the last step together and celebrate.
DIY Alternative
Draw simple stick figures on index cards. Take photos of your child at each stage of the slide approach (even if they don't complete it) and print them as a personalized sequence. Personalized imagery is more effective than generic images.
Material 9 of 9
Backyard or Indoor Mini Slide
What It Is
A small plastic toddler slide (typically 3–4 feet tall maximum) installed in a familiar, low-stakes home environment where the child has maximum control, caregiver presence, and the ability to approach on their own timeline.
Why It Works
The home environment removes the social pressure, unfamiliarity, and sensory overwhelm of a public playground. The child can approach the mini slide dozens of times per day — sitting near it, touching it, climbing partway, observing it — without the performance anxiety that public settings create. Proximity and repeated non-threatening exposure is a core principle of desensitization. Over 2–6 weeks of daily low-pressure exposure, most children with mild-to-moderate gravitational insecurity transition from avoidance to independent use of the mini slide — which then becomes a template for the larger playground slide.
How to Use It — Step by Step
  1. Install mini slide in a preferred room (living room, playroom). Do not immediately direct child toward it.
  1. Week 1: Allow passive exposure — child plays near slide, parent makes no comments about the slide itself.
  1. Week 2: Model using the slide yourself. Narrate your experience: "That was so smooth and fast! My body felt the whoosh!"
  1. Week 3: Invite (never push) child to sit at the bottom. Then sit at the top. Then go partway down with parent support.
  1. Week 4+: Child completes independent slide run. Celebrate enthusiastically.
DIY Alternative
Ramp board (Material 5) plus a small step stool — combining a 6-inch elevation with the ramp angle creates a functional mini-slide analog for under $20.
Safety Note
Ensure mini slide is on non-slip flooring or anchored per manufacturer instructions. Supervise all use for children under age 4.

ACT IV — THE HOME PROTOCOL
The Step-by-Step Home Protocol
This protocol translates the clinical framework into a practical, week-by-week plan that parents and caregivers can implement at home — with or without a formal OT referral in place. It is designed for children presenting with mild-to-moderate gravitational insecurity. For significant-to-severe presentations, use this as an OT carryover protocol, not a standalone intervention.
Duration
6–12 weeks for mild. 12–24 weeks for moderate. Progress is nonlinear — expect plateaus and regressions.
Frequency
Daily 10–15 minute sessions are more effective than weekly 60-minute sessions. Consistency over intensity.
Core Principle
Never force. Always invite. Follow the child's nervous system, not the clock or the other children on the playground.
Week 1–2: Safety and Trust Building
Before any movement work begins, the nervous system needs to establish that the adult caregiver is a reliable source of safety — not a source of pressure. This phase cannot be skipped, even if it feels unproductive. It is the foundation everything else builds on.
Establish the "Stop Signal"
Teach a clear, child-controlled stop signal ("red light," hand up, or specific word). Practice it in completely safe, non-threatening contexts so it becomes automatic. The child must know they can always stop anything at any time.
Introduce Body Awareness Language
Begin narrating body sensations during comfortable activities: "I notice my feet are firmly on the floor. My body feels calm and heavy." Builds interoceptive awareness that will be used during vestibular activities.
Proprioceptive Play Daily
Every day: 10 minutes of heavy work (pushing, pulling, carrying, climbing low structures). This organizes the nervous system and increases vestibular tolerance over time. No slides required yet.
Read the Slide Story
Introduce the visual schedule/slide story (Material 8). Read it together as a bedtime book — no agenda, no pressure. Just familiarization with the narrative.
Week 3–4: Passive Vestibular Input
Introduce the body to movement in its most controlled, predictable form. The child is passenger, not driver. The nervous system experiences movement without the demand of initiating or controlling it — reducing the cognitive and emotional load significantly.
Platform Swing Sessions
5–10 minutes of linear swing movement. Child sits or lies. Parent controls all motion. Begin with zero movement, add movement only with verbal consent. Use body sock beforehand if available.
Therapy Ball Work
Seated ball balancing with parent hand support. 5 minutes before or after swing. Narrate: "Feel how your body is making tiny adjustments? Your nervous system is learning."
Blanket Swinging
Child lies in blanket hammock held by two adults. Slow linear sway only. Add playful sound effects ("whoooosh") to create positive emotional association with movement sensation.
Victory Journaling
At end of each session, note one specific success — no matter how small. "Today she sat in the swing for 2 full minutes without asking to get off." These notes become powerful motivators during plateau periods.
Week 5–6: Active Ground-Level Movement
The child now begins to initiate and control movement — still at floor level, still without height. This phase builds the child's sense of agency over their own vestibular experience, which is neurologically critical. Control is the antidote to fear.
In this phase, the child should be completing 3–5 successful ramp board runs per session before progression. "Successful" means the child initiates the movement independently and does not show distress at the bottom. Smiling, laughing, or requesting "again!" are the target indicators.

Regression Note: If the child regresses during this phase (common after illness, life transitions, or stressful weeks), return to Week 3–4 activities without judgment. Say: "Let's do the cozy swing today." Never frame regression as failure.
Week 7–8: Graduated Elevation Introduction
For the first time, height enters the picture — but in the most controlled, incremental way possible. The goal is not the full slide. The goal is 3 inches of elevation today, 6 inches next week, 12 inches the week after. Each new height is introduced only after the previous height has been mastered with spontaneous, self-initiated engagement.
6-Inch Elevation
Ramp board elevated at one end by a thick book or stable block. Child slides from 6 inches above floor. Parent contact maintained at all times.
12-Inch Elevation
Ramp elevated to stair-height or low step height. Introduce the mini slide (Material 9) at its lowest setting if available.
18–24-Inch Elevation
Mini slide at standard height. Child approaches, climbs, and slides independently with parent standing at the side (not at the bottom — positioning matters for vestibular confidence).
30+ Inch Elevation
Transition-readiness point. Child who confidently completes 30-inch mini slide at home is neurologically ready to attempt the smallest playground slide with parent nearby.
Week 9–10: The First Playground Attempt
Choose the playground visit deliberately. Timing matters enormously. Go during off-peak hours (early morning on a weekday) when the playground is nearly empty. Bring the visual schedule. Wear the weighted vest. Pack a favorite snack for after. Set a single, specific, achievable goal — not "go down the slide" but "touch the bottom of the slide ladder."
Pre-Visit Preparation
Read the slide story the night before and morning of. Do 5 minutes of proprioceptive heavy work before leaving home. Dress child in weighted vest before entering the playground.
At the Playground
Don't go directly to the slide. Play freely for 10–15 minutes first. Let the child lead. When they're regulated and comfortable, approach the smallest slide together — no agenda, just proximity.
The Attempt
Invite, don't push: "Do you want to try one step on the ladder?" Accept any answer. If the child touches the ladder, that is a success. If they go up one step, that is a triumph. If they slide down, celebrate as if they won an Olympic medal.
The Language That Helps vs. The Language That Harms
The words used during vestibular desensitization work are not incidental — they are clinical variables. Language shapes the child's narrative about their own nervous system. These phrases have been tested and refined by the Pinnacle Blooms Consortium across hundreds of clinical and home sessions.
Language That Helps ✓
  • "Your body is learning something new. That takes time."
  • "You are so brave — brave means doing it even when it's scary."
  • "Tell me what your body is feeling right now."
  • "We don't have to go down today. We can just be near it."
  • "I'll be right here. My hands are on you the whole time."
  • "What would help your body feel safer right now?"
  • "Look how far you came — remember when we couldn't even walk up to it?"
Language to Avoid ✗
  • "It's just a slide — there's nothing to be scared of."
  • "Look, even the little kids are doing it."
  • "You're being silly about this."
  • "Just try it — I promise you'll love it."
  • "Stop crying and go down."
  • "We came all this way, you HAVE to try."
  • "You're embarrassing yourself."

Clinical Rationale: Invalidating language activates the amygdala — the opposite of what desensitization requires. Even well-intentioned pressure phrases ("I promise it's safe!") trigger increased vigilance. The nervous system hears urgency, not reassurance.
When a Session Goes Sideways
There will be sessions — possibly many of them — where the plan fails completely. The child melts down at the playground entrance. They were doing so well and then suddenly refused. They had three perfect sessions and now won't even look at the mini slide. This is not failure. This is the nervous system working exactly as it is supposed to work during desensitization.
What to Do in the Moment
Stop immediately. Use the stop signal the child already knows. Move to a calming activity (body sock, proprioceptive work, or quiet sitting together). Never process the event in the moment — wait until the nervous system is calm.
What to Say After
"That was a hard day for your body. Your nervous system needed more time. That's okay — we'll try again when your body is ready." Do not express disappointment or frustration, even subtly through sighing or silence.
Adjusting the Protocol
If sessions are consistently difficult, step back one full phase in the protocol. Spend an additional week at the previous level before progressing again. More time at a lower level builds a stronger foundation for sustainable progress.
When to Seek OT Support
If the child is not making any observable progress after 8 weeks of consistent home protocol implementation, or if anxiety is generalizing to new contexts, request a formal occupational therapy evaluation for sensory integration.
Tracking Progress: What Success Actually Looks Like
In desensitization work, progress is subtle before it is dramatic. Parents who track specific behavioral indicators — rather than the single metric of "did they go down the slide?" — see progress much earlier and maintain motivation through plateau periods. Use these markers as your clinical compass.

Approach Distance
Child gets within 25% of slide distance without distress — progress, even without touching it

ACT V — THE CLINICAL VOICE
The Therapist's Guide to This Protocol
For pediatric OTs, SLPs, BCBAs, and special educators: this section translates the parent-facing protocol into clinical language appropriate for program planning, progress documentation, and IEP/IFSP goal alignment. The 9 materials described above map directly to sensory integration treatment domains established by Ayres (1972) and updated through the current evidence base.
Assessment Integration
Gravitational insecurity can be formally assessed using the Sensory Integration and Praxis Tests (SIPT), the Sensory Processing Measure (SPM-2), or the Sensory Profile 2. Establish a baseline before beginning the protocol.
Goal Writing
Sample IEP goal: "Child will independently approach and descend a standard playground slide (minimum 48 inches) without adult physical assistance, demonstrating regulated affect, in 3 of 5 consecutive opportunities across 2 different playground settings."
Progress Monitoring
Measure proximity tolerance (distance from slide without distress), grip intensity (1–5 scale), and verbal expression of fear (0 = no verbal fear expression, 5 = immediate meltdown) at each session. Graph weekly.
Dosing and Frequency: Clinical Recommendations
Evidence from sensory integration research consistently points to the same finding: frequency of exposure matters more than duration of individual sessions for vestibular desensitization. This has direct implications for how therapists structure treatment and caregiver carryover.
In-Clinic Dosing
  • 2x/week minimum for meaningful vestibular change
  • Session length: 45–60 minutes (20 min proprioceptive prep + 15 min vestibular work + 10 min integration)
  • Vestibular activities: 10–15 minutes maximum within each session
  • Rest between challenges: minimum 2 minutes regulated breathing
Home Carryover Dosing
  • Daily 10–15 minute sessions (5 days/week minimum)
  • Brief vestibular activities are more effective than weekend-only long sessions
  • Proprioceptive heavy work: 20 minutes daily, integrated into natural routines
  • Playground visits: 2x/week, using the graduated approach protocol

Research Basis: Sensory integration intervention delivers best outcomes with high dosage. Cohn et al. (2021) found 30+ hours of SI therapy across 16 weeks produced the most significant functional gains. Home carryover doubles the effective dose without additional cost.
Co-Occurring Conditions: Clinical Context
Gravitational insecurity rarely presents in isolation. Understanding the child's full neurodevelopmental profile allows therapists to sequence interventions appropriately and anticipate barriers. The following co-occurring conditions are commonly seen alongside vestibular over-responsivity and slide-specific fear.
Autism Spectrum Disorder
Present in approximately 80% of cases with significant sensory processing differences. Social motivation may be lower, reducing peer-modeling effectiveness. Visual supports (Material 8) are especially important. Predictability is paramount.
Generalized Anxiety
Vestibular insecurity and anxiety co-activate through the amygdala. Treat anxiety as a parallel intervention track. Cognitive-behavioral strategies (age-adapted thought records, worry time) complement the sensory protocol.
Developmental Coordination Disorder
Motor planning challenges increase the functional difficulty of the slide experience (climbing, positioning, initiating the slide). Add explicit motor sequencing practice — "foot here, hand here, sit down, scoot" — as a parallel strand.
ADHD
Impulsivity can paradoxically co-exist with vestibular avoidance — the child may impulsively attempt the slide and then panic mid-way. Prepare for this: practice the "stop at the top" pause before initiating descent.

Frequently Asked Questions
These questions reflect the most common concerns raised by parents, caregivers, and therapists navigating slide fear and gravitational insecurity. The answers reflect the Pinnacle Blooms Consortium's clinical consensus as of March 2026.
Will my child simply grow out of this on their own?
Some children with mild gravitational insecurity do show spontaneous improvement as the vestibular system matures. However, without intervention, moderate-to-severe presentations typically persist and often generalize — leading to avoidance of sports, PE class, escalators, and social settings involving movement. Early intervention produces dramatically better outcomes than watchful waiting.
Is this the same as exposure therapy for phobias?
Graduated vestibular desensitization shares principles with CBT-based exposure therapy but is distinct: it directly targets the sensory system, not just the cognitive fear response. It is sensory-first (proprioceptive loading before vestibular challenge) rather than cognition-first. Both approaches benefit from integration, and collaboration between OT and a child psychologist produces the best outcomes for moderate-severe presentations.
How do I explain this to my child's school?
Request a sensory assessment through the school's occupational therapist. Frame the request around functional participation: "My child is unable to participate in recess activities due to movement-related anxiety. I'd like a sensory evaluation to determine whether accommodations or direct OT services are warranted."
More FAQs: Practical Parent Questions
What if my child has a meltdown every time we try?
This signals the protocol is starting too advanced. Return to Phase 1 entirely: no slides, no ramps, no playground. Spend 2–3 weeks doing only proprioceptive heavy work and passive swing at home. Build the nervous system's regulatory foundation before reintroducing any incline or elevation challenge. Consistency at a low level always beats attempting a higher level prematurely.
Is this fear hurting my child developmentally?
Playground avoidance has documented downstream effects on physical fitness, peer socialization, motor development, and self-confidence. Children who avoid playground equipment miss critical opportunities for proprioceptive and vestibular development that typically occur through free play. Addressing slide fear is not cosmetic — it has meaningful developmental stakes.
Can siblings help with this protocol?
Yes — sibling modeling is one of the most potent tools available. An older sibling demonstrating joy on the ramp board or mini slide while narrating the experience ("it feels like swoooosh!") activates the child's mirror neuron system and creates positive associations. Peer modeling within families is highly effective, provided the sibling is coached not to pressure or tease.
