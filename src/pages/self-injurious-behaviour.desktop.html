D-401A · Understanding Self-Injury
You Don't Know Why. That's Where We Start.
Your child is hurting themselves. Deliberately. Repeatedly. Hitting their own head. Biting their own hand. You're terrified, helpless, and no one prepared you for this. You don't know whether to stop it, ignore it, or rush to the hospital. You feel like the worst parent alive. You're not. And you're not alone — self-injurious behaviour (SIB) occurs in 25–50% of children with ASD.
SIB is not self-harm in the psychiatric sense. It is not suicidal behaviour. It is not a sign of bad parenting or psychosis. SIB is a neurological response to an internal state — sensory need, emotional overwhelm, a communication deficit, or undetected pain — that the child cannot resolve through any other available behaviour. The body becomes the only tool they have left.

⚠️ Why punishment worsens SIB: Punishment increases distress → increased distress increases the need for the endorphin release SIB provides → SIB increases. Punishment creates the exact internal state that SIB exists to manage.
📊 Level I Evidence
Functional analysis (Iwata et al.) is the gold standard for SIB assessment. Function-based intervention produces 80–90% reduction in SIB frequency.
🏥 Multi-Disciplinary
BCBA + Psychology + Medical + OT working together produces the best outcomes. No single discipline can manage SIB alone.
⚠️ Pain First
Always rule out ear infection, toothache, constipation, reflux, or other medical cause BEFORE any behavioural intervention begins.
Evidence: NCAEP 2020 | Iwata et al. 1994 | BACB | PMC9978394
D-401B · Understanding Self-Injury
Understanding Self-Injury — What You'll Do
Before any technique can help, caregivers and professionals must develop a clear, accurate mental model of SIB. This is the foundation card — everything that follows builds on this understanding. The detective approach asks: WHEN does it happen? WHERE on the body? WHO is present? WHAT preceded it? What STOPS it?
The Three SIB Types
Sensory (calm, rhythmic, alone or with others) · Emotional (distressed, irregular, during overwhelm or meltdowns) · Communication (social context, produces a response, needs are unmet)
⚠️ Rule Out Pain First
Sudden onset or increase in SIB may indicate: ear infection, toothache, constipation, reflux, headache, UTI, fracture. Medical evaluation before behavioural intervention — always.
Emergency Signs
Vomiting, unequal pupils, loss of consciousness, blood from ears/nose, sudden lethargy, eye swelling or redness → Emergency care immediately.
For Parents
This is NOT your fault. You did not cause this. Your presence, your love, and your willingness to learn — these are already the most powerful interventions available.

D-402A · Head Banging
Head Against Wall. Head Against Floor. The Most Terrifying SIB.
The sound. The thud. The red mark forming. Head against wall, head against floor, head against table — every impact sends shockwaves through the skull. You're watching your child strike the most vulnerable part of their body, and every instinct tells you to hold their head and never let go. You're right to be frightened. Head banging requires urgent assessment.
The Neuroscience
Head banging provides massive proprioceptive and vestibular input simultaneously. The skull impact generates intense sensory feedback through both systems at once — producing potential endorphin release and a temporary neurochemical reset. The child may also be communicating: "I'm in distress and I have no other way to tell you."
⚠️ Critical Pain Indicator
Head banging can indicate PAIN — especially ear infection, toothache, sinus pressure, or headache. The child may be hitting the LOCATION of the pain. New-onset or increased head banging requires medical evaluation first, before any behavioural strategy is introduced.
⚠️ Concussion Risk
Repeated head banging can cause concussion, subdural haematoma, skull fractures, and retinal haemorrhage. Signs requiring EMERGENCY care: vomiting, unequal pupils, loss of consciousness, blood from ears or nose, sudden lethargy.
Evidence: Level I — FBA + medical evaluation + protective equipment + matched replacement. NCAEP 2020 | Iwata
D-402B · Head Banging
Head Banging — What You'll Do
Safety comes first — always. Blocking the impact (hand between head and surface, never restraining), redirecting to a soft surface, and clearing hard surfaces during escalation are your immediate tools. Then the real work begins: understanding why it's happening and building a proactive plan.
Step 1: Medical Evaluation
Rule out ear infection, dental pain, sinus pressure, headache, and constipation. Head banging that begins suddenly or intensifies unexpectedly is a medical signal until proven otherwise.
Step 2: Surface Safety
Foam tiles on walls and floors in danger zones. Budget-friendly Indian options: old mattress against wall, foam camping mats, folded blankets, padded headboards. These are harm reduction — not treatment.
Step 3: High-Intensity Alternatives
Crash pad diving, trampoline, heavy blanket compression, vibrating pillow against head, bear hugs, joint compressions — proactively scheduled every 60–90 minutes BEFORE the child seeks input through head banging.
Step 4: Helmet Decision
When necessary and criteria-appropriate — a temporary protective measure reviewed every 2–4 weeks by the BCBA and medical team. Never a permanent solution or punishment.

D-403A · Self-Biting
The Hand Is Always Available — Making Self-Biting the Most Accessible SIB
Teeth on own hand, wrist, forearm. Bite marks. Calluses. Open wounds. The jaw is one of the strongest joints in the body relative to its size — and the hand is always within reach. Chronic self-biters develop calluses that reduce pain sensitivity, so they bite harder to achieve the same input. This creates a dangerous escalation cycle that produces progressively more tissue damage if not interrupted early.

⚠️ The escalation cycle: Bite → callus forms → reduced sensation → bite harder → more callus → even harder → tissue damage deepens. Intervention MUST break this cycle early before it becomes self-reinforcing at a higher intensity level.
Oral Proprioception
The TMJ is one of the highest proprioceptive-density joints in the body. Self-biting delivers intense, deep joint input that the nervous system craves.
Endorphin Release
Pain threshold triggers an opioid cascade. The child may experience genuine neurochemical relief — making the behaviour powerfully self-reinforcing.
Communication Signal
"I need help NOW." When frustration peaks and no other communication system works fast enough, the jaw delivers the message instantly.
Evidence: Level I — Oral sensory programme + FBA + FCT. NCAEP 2020
D-403B · Self-Biting
Self-Biting — What You'll Do
The cardinal rule: a chew tool must be accessible everywhere, always — lanyard around neck, on the desk, in every room, in the car, in the school bag. When self-biting occurs, gently present the chew tool at the mouth while saying "Bite this." Do NOT pull the hand forcefully from the mouth — this can cause jaw injury or tooth damage.
Chew Tool Protocol
ARK Therapeutic tools, chew necklaces, vibrating chew tools — matched to the child's bite force. Soft, medium, and hard durometer options exist. Choose based on bite intensity observed during clinical assessment.
Oral Sensory Diet
Crunchy foods (carrots, apples, papad, murukku), vibrating toothbrush on gums, chewing gum, oral motor exercises — scheduled throughout the day, not only during SIB episodes. Proactive, not reactive.
Function-Specific Response
Sensory biting → chew schedule. Emotional biting → co-regulation first, then chew. Communication biting → FCT ("Help" card) paired with chew tool access.
Wound Care
Antiseptic cleaning, bandaging, infection monitoring. Broken skin requires medical attention — infection risk is significant. Arm guards or protective sleeves are temporary measures while FCT is being taught.

D-404A · Self-Hitting
The Fight Response Turned Inward
Hitting own face with open palm. Punching own head with closed fist. Slapping own legs. Striking own chest. When the fight response cannot go outward — toward others — it goes inward. The aggression circuit turns against the self. This is one of the most emotionally distressing forms of SIB for caregivers to witness precisely because the child is both the source and the target of their own distress.
The location reveals the function. Face and head: maximum sensory input (highest nerve density) or pain indication (headache, toothache, ear). Legs and body: proprioceptive seeking in large muscle groups. Chest: emotional distress expression — the visceral heart-area connection to feeling overwhelmed. Arms: frustration discharge. Reading the location helps the team identify the correct intervention approach.
Proprioceptive Input
Impact produces intense deep pressure feedback across the struck area — input the nervous system is urgently seeking and cannot obtain another way.
Emotional Discharge
The motor system MUST release the energy of the fight response. Self-hitting is the body completing a biological imperative with no other outlet available.
Endorphin Activation
Repeated impact triggers a beta-endorphin cascade — the brain's own painkiller system activates, providing temporary neurochemical relief from overwhelming distress.
Evidence: Level I — FBA + matched proprioceptive replacement. NCAEP 2020
D-404B · Self-Hitting
Self-Hitting — What You'll Do
Immediate response: redirect the hand gently — guide it toward a squeeze ball, crash pad, or pillow. Do NOT grab and hold hands down. Restraint increases distress, which increases the drive toward SIB. Instead, provide firm deep pressure to the area that was being struck — this delivers the sensory input the nervous system needs without causing damage.
Face-Hitting Alternatives
Vibrating pillow against face, firm bilateral face press with hands, deep pressure face massage. Match the intensity — light touch will not substitute for impact.
Head-Hitting Alternatives
Crash pad diving, heavy work activities, joint compressions, weighted hat for sustained proprioceptive input to the head and neck region.
Leg-Hitting Alternatives
Stomping, weighted lap pad, leg squeezes, seated bouncing on therapy ball — delivering deep proprioception to the lower extremities proactively.
Body-Hitting Alternatives
Compression vest, bear hugs, body sock — full-body proprioceptive input that meets the emotional discharge need without self-directed impact.

⚠️ Proactive heavy work schedule: every 90 minutes — wall pushes, carrying heavy bags, jumping, crawling, pushing furniture. Schedule BEFORE SIB occurs, not only in response to it. Facial bruising or swelling → medical evaluation required.

D-405A · Self-Scratching
It May Look Like Itching — But It Doesn't Stop When the Itch Is Gone
Scratching own arms, face, and legs — until red, until bleeding, until permanently scarred. The rhythmic, repetitive nature of self-scratching can become fully automatic through basal ganglia habituation, running without conscious awareness during screen time, during waiting, during anxiety, or during boredom. The child may not even know they are doing it.
Self-scratching provides tactile stimulation, potential endorphin release, visual feedback (watching the skin change — redness, marks appearing), and anxiety discharge. Before any behavioural intervention: rule out a dermatological cause. In India's climate, eczema, heat rash, prickly heat, fungal infection, mosquito bite reactions, and contact dermatitis are all common. A dermatologist must evaluate the child before a BCBA develops any behaviour plan.
⚠️ Dermatological First
Is there a physical itch driving this behaviour? Eczema, allergic reaction, dry skin, fungal infection, scabies, contact dermatitis — especially relevant in India's diverse climate conditions.
Habitual Automaticity
The basal ganglia runs this pattern on autopilot. Habit reversal — not just distraction — is required. The child needs awareness training before any competing response can take hold.
Evidence: Level I — Dermatological evaluation + FBA + tactile replacement. NCAEP 2020
D-405B · Self-Scratching
Self-Scratching — What You'll Do
Start with nails. Keep them short, filed smooth, and rounded — schedule nail trimming weekly, treating it like medication. Long sleeves and protective clothing during vulnerable periods reduce both access and visual scanning for scratch targets. These are immediate management tools while the comprehensive plan develops.
Tactile Replacements
Textured bracelets to rub, velcro strips on desk or chair arm, sensory fabric squares in pockets, koosh balls, textured phone cases — items that satisfy the same skin-sensation drive through a safe channel, always within reach.
Skin Care as Prevention
Daily moisturising with coconut oil or prescribed cream, gentle exfoliation to reduce rough skin "targets," bandaging healing wounds to remove visual triggers and reduce infection risk, keeping skin hydrated in India's variable climate.
Habit Awareness Training
Teaching the child to NOTICE when scratching begins — wearable tracker, parent verbal cue "hands check." Then a competing response: clasping hands, gripping a fidget, pressing palms together firmly.
Address Underlying Anxiety
When self-scratching is anxiety-driven, addressing the anxiety (C2 domain link) is as important as the tactile replacement. Both layers must be treated simultaneously.

D-406A · Hair Pulling (Own)
Fistfuls From the Scalp. Single Strands Between Fingers. Bald Patches Forming.
Pulling their own hair — from the scalp, eyebrows, eyelashes. Distinct from pulling others' hair, this is entirely self-directed. Trichotillomania in the ASD context sits at the intersection of sensory seeking, habitual behaviour, and anxiety — a body-focused repetitive behaviour (BFRB) that often intensifies during idle time, concentration, anxiety, or the transition to sleep.
In the Indian cultural context, hair carries deep significance. Bald patches draw family attention, comments from relatives, and shame that feeds directly back into the anxiety that drives more pulling. The shame cycle must be interrupted as part of treatment. Hair pulling → bald patch → shame → more anxiety → more pulling. The family's response is part of the intervention.
Scalp Proprioception
The scalp is richly innervated. Pulling tension delivers intense proprioceptive input through the scalp — a sensation the nervous system urgently seeks.
Finger Tactile Sensation
The sliding, twisting, and rolling of hair between fingers delivers fine-motor tactile feedback — satisfying and habitual, often occurring without awareness.
Completion Drive
The hair MUST come out — a pattern completion drive similar to D-378. The anterior cingulate cortex signals "this is incomplete" until the strand releases.
Evidence: Level I — Habit reversal + sensory replacement + anxiety management. NCAEP 2020 | Trichotillomania research
D-406B · Hair Pulling (Own)
Hair Pulling — What You'll Do
The key to intervention is identifying WHEN pulling occurs. Idle time → provide hand occupation. Anxiety → address anxiety directly. Concentration → provide an alternative fidget. Sleep onset → consider gloves or a soft bandana at bedtime to reduce access to pulling sites. The context determines the strategy.
Tactile Alternatives for Fingers
Koosh balls, pipe cleaners, textured keychains, Tangle toys, silly putty, threading beads, string wrapping — items that occupy the fingers with similar fine-motor tactile input, available at all high-risk times.
Scalp-Sensation Alternatives
Head massage, vibrating scalp massager, tight headband (proprioceptive input to the scalp without the pulling action). Satisfies the scalp tension need through a non-damaging channel.
Habit Reversal Protocol
(1) Awareness training — "your hand is in your hair" prompt. (2) Competing response — fist-clench or squeeze toy when urge arises. (3) Social support — gentle redirection without shame or punishment.
Bald Patch Management
Medical evaluation for regrowth potential, dermatology referral, managing family and social reactions compassionately. Caps, headbands, and braids can temporarily reduce access to primary pulling sites.

⚠️ Severe trichotillomania warrants Psychology referral and possible OCD assessment. Hair pulling that is intrusive, distress-driven, or accompanied by obsessive thoughts requires specialist evaluation beyond the scope of home strategies.

D-407A · Skin Picking
The Wound Heals Into a Scab. The Scab Becomes the Next Target.
Picking at scabs, cuticles, lips, any skin imperfection — until bleeding. Excoriation disorder in the ASD context creates its own perpetual cycle: picking creates wounds that create scabs that create MORE picking targets. Arms, face, and hands become marked with a landscape of scars and active wounds. The cycle is self-sustaining until deliberately broken.
The neuroscience is precise: the anterior cingulate cortex fires "this is wrong — fix it" every time an imperfection is detected on the skin. The picking temporarily silences this alarm, producing relief. Then healing creates a new scab — a new "wrong" — and the alarm fires again. The brain creates its own triggers. Visual fascination with the changing skin — the scab lifting, the redness appearing — adds a reinforcing visual feedback loop to the tactile one.
ACC Completion Drive
The imperfection MUST be removed. The anterior cingulate cortex fires an alarm signal until the bump, scab, or dry skin patch is gone — a powerful, automatic neurological imperative.
Tactile + Visual Reinforcement
Fine-motor picking feedback plus watching the skin change creates a dual reinforcement loop — particularly resistant to extinction without matched alternatives.
Anxiety Relief
For many children, picking functions as anxiety regulation — a repetitive, focused, calming action that reduces physiological arousal temporarily.
Evidence: Level I — Habit reversal + skin care + fidget replacement. NCAEP 2020
D-407B · Skin Picking
Skin Picking — What You'll Do
The most effective first step is breaking the pick-wound-scab-pick cycle at the wound level. Heal existing wounds with antiseptic and bandages — this removes the visual target that drives continued picking. Daily moisturising reduces the dry skin "triggers" that the ACC detects and alarms over. Cover picking sites and cover the arms during high-risk periods: idle time, screen time, anxiety.
Fidget Replacements
Bubble pop toys, peeling stickers off sheets, peeling tape, popping bubble wrap, picking at playdough or clay, picking beads from putty — these satisfy the SAME fine-motor picking pattern through a safe, non-damaging channel.
Covering Strategy
Bandages over active picking sites, long sleeves, gloves during high-risk times. Removing the visual "target" from sight reduces the frequency of ACC alarm triggers significantly.
Skin Care Routine
Daily moisturising (coconut oil or prescribed lotion), gentle exfoliation, sunscreen on healed areas to reduce scar visibility. Prevention reduces available "targets" over time.
Habit Awareness
"Hands check" prompts from caregivers, wearable reminder bracelet on the picking hand, visual cue cards near high-risk sitting areas — building moment-to-moment awareness before competing responses can activate.

⚠️ Infected picking wounds → medical attention immediately. OCD or anxiety assessment needed if picking is distress-driven, intrusive, or accompanied by obsessive thoughts about imperfection.

D-408A · Eye Pressing
⚠️ Highest Medical Urgency
⚠️ Eye Pressing — The Highest Medical Urgency SIB in This Entire System
Pressing fingers or fists into the eyes. Pressing objects against the eyes. Poking at the eyes. This SIB carries the highest risk of irreversible harm of any behaviour in the entire 999-technique system. Act immediately.

⚠️ Potential consequences of untreated eye pressing: Retinal detachment · Lens displacement · Keratoconus (corneal deformation) · Orbital tissue damage · Enophthalmos (sunken eyes) · Permanent vision loss. These are not rare outcomes — they occur in children whose eye pressing goes unaddressed.
The neuroscience explains the drive: eye pressing mechanically stimulates the retina, producing phosphenes — visual light patterns, colours, and shapes generated directly within the visual system. The child is creating a personal visual light show. For children who are visual-seeking or who have cortical visual impairment — where the brain processes less visual input than needed — eye pressing may be the most intense, most accessible visual stimulation available.
Phosphene Generation
Direct mechanical stimulation of the retina creates colours, patterns, and light shapes — vivid visual experience generated from within, bypassing the need for external visual input.
Visual Impairment Link
More common in children with visual impairment + ASD, cortical visual impairment, or blindness (oculodigital sign of Franceschetti). The phosphenes provide visual experience when the visual system is impaired.
Evidence: Level I — URGENT ophthalmological evaluation + visual replacement + protective strategies. NCAEP 2020 | Ophthalmology in ASD
D-408B · Eye Pressing
Eye Pressing — Urgent Steps and What You'll Do
Ophthalmological evaluation must happen immediately for every child who presses their eyes — without exception. Document frequency, force applied, and any visible signs of eye changes. A full vision assessment is essential to determine whether underlying visual impairment is the primary driver of the behaviour.
Urgent: Ophthalmology
Immediate eye evaluation for all eye-pressing children. Vision assessment. Document frequency and force. Check for signs of existing damage.
Visual Replacements
Fibre optic lights, LED spinning toys, lava lamps, kaleidoscopes, light projectors, glitter bottles, disco balls, light tables, colour-changing LED strips — intense visual stimulation WITHOUT eye contact.
Hand Redirection
Gentle hand removal paired with IMMEDIATE visual toy presentation. Teach "look at THIS" while presenting the light alternative. Timing is critical — the replacement must appear within 1–2 seconds.
Environmental Enrichment
Create a visually stimulating environment that reduces the unmet need driving self-generated phosphenes. OT visual processing assessment and intervention is mandatory.

⚠️ Signs requiring EMERGENCY response: redness, swelling, discharge, vision changes, unequal pupil size, pain response when the eye area is touched. Go to emergency immediately — do not wait for the next clinic appointment.

D-409A · Sensory-Based SIB
The Child Looks Calm. That's What Makes Sensory SIB So Hard to Treat.
The sensory SIB master card. SIB that occurs because the nervous system has an extremely high threshold for proprioceptive and tactile input — so high that only self-injury reaches it. The defining features: the child is CALM during the SIB. It occurs regardless of social context — alone, in company, during activity, during idle time. It's rhythmic, predictable, and habitual. The child doesn't look distressed — they look like they're feeding a need. Because they are.
Why This Is the Hardest to Treat
The reinforcement is INTERNAL — it cannot be controlled by others. The intensity threshold is extremely high — alternatives must genuinely match it. The behaviour is AUTOMATIC — habitual, running without conscious choice. Three layers of treatment resistance simultaneously.
The Neural Mechanism
Sensory SIB = automatic reinforcement. The somatosensory cortex requires input intensity the environment cannot provide through safe activities. The basal ganglia runs the SIB pattern on autopilot. The nucleus accumbens may provide endogenous reward — the SIB can genuinely feel good to the child's nervous system.
Evidence: Level I — Sensory assessment + matched high-intensity alternatives + maximum sensory diet. NCAEP 2020
D-409B · Sensory-Based SIB
Sensory-Based SIB — What You'll Do
The intervention logic is simple but the execution is hard: you must find a safe activity that delivers the SAME intensity of sensory input as the SIB. Not similar. Not close. The same. Anything less will fail — the nervous system will reject it and return to the SIB that actually works.
Sensory Assessment First
A qualified OT with sensory integration training must map the child's sensory profile. Which systems are under-responsive? Proprioceptive? Vestibular? Tactile? Deep pressure? The replacement must target the exact same system the SIB is feeding.
Build a Proactive Sensory Diet
Schedule high-intensity sensory input every 60–90 minutes throughout the day — BEFORE the child reaches the threshold that triggers SIB. Crash pad diving, trampoline, joint compressions, bear hugs, weighted vest, vibration tools. Proactive, not reactive.
Match the Intensity
If the SIB is head banging, the replacement must be equally intense: crash pad, wall push-ups, heavy work. If the SIB is self-biting, the replacement must match jaw pressure: chew tools rated for high-intensity chewing, not soft silicone. Mismatched intensity = failed replacement.
Environmental Saturation
Make the sensory environment so rich that the nervous system's threshold is met before SIB is needed. Sensory corners, movement breaks, tactile stations, vibration tools throughout the day. The goal is a nervous system that is never starved.

D-410A · Pica
They're Eating It. Not Food. Deliberately. Repeatedly.
Pica — the persistent ingestion of non-nutritive, non-food substances. Dirt, paper, cloth, paint chips, hair, soap, faeces, small objects. In children with ASD and intellectual disability, pica is one of the most medically dangerous behaviours in this entire system. It is not curiosity. It is not a phase. It is a behaviour with a function — and it requires urgent medical and behavioural attention simultaneously.
⚠️ Medical Urgency
Pica can cause intestinal obstruction, intestinal perforation, lead poisoning (paint chips, soil), parasitic infection (soil, faeces), choking and asphyxiation, toxic poisoning, and internal bleeding. Every child with pica requires immediate paediatric gastroenterology and toxicology evaluation. This is not optional.
Why Pica Occurs
Pica in ASD/ID has multiple functions: sensory (oral stimulation, texture, taste), nutritional deficiency (iron, zinc — the body seeks what it lacks), automatic reinforcement (the ingestion itself is rewarding), and occasionally escape or attention. Iron-deficiency anaemia is strongly associated with pica — blood work is mandatory before any behavioural plan begins.
Evidence: Level I — Medical evaluation mandatory + FBA + oral sensory replacement + environmental safety. NCAEP 2020 | JABA
D-410B · Pica
Pica — What You'll Do
Pica management operates on two simultaneous tracks: medical stabilisation and environmental safety first, then behavioural intervention. Neither track can be skipped. A behaviour plan without medical clearance is dangerous. Medical treatment without a behaviour plan will not stop the behaviour.
Environmental Safety
Remove or secure all pica targets from the environment. This is harm reduction, not treatment. Locked cabinets, supervision protocols, removal of accessible non-food items. In India's homes: secure soil-filled pots, paint, chalk, small decorative items, incense ash. Supervision must be constant during the intervention phase.
Nutritional Workup
Blood panel for iron, zinc, calcium, and haemoglobin. If deficiency is found, supplementation may reduce pica significantly. This is one of the few cases where a medical intervention directly reduces a behaviour. Do not skip this step.
Oral Sensory Replacement
Provide safe oral stimulation that matches the texture and sensation of the pica target. Crunchy foods, chewy foods, textured chew tools, flavoured chew necklaces. The oral system needs input — give it a safe channel. Work with OT to identify the specific oral sensory profile.
Discrimination Training
Teach the child to discriminate between food and non-food items. Use visual supports, social stories, and repeated practice. This is a long-term skill-building goal, not an immediate safety measure.

D-411A · Self-Pinching
The Fingers Find Skin. They Twist. They Don't Let Go.
Self-pinching — fingers gripping and twisting skin on the arms, thighs, abdomen, face, or neck. Often leaving bruises, welts, and broken capillaries. Unlike head banging or self-biting, self-pinching is quiet. It can happen in plain sight without anyone noticing. The child's face may remain completely neutral. This invisibility makes it one of the most under-documented SIBs in clinical practice.
Why Self-Pinching Occurs
Self-pinching provides intense, localised proprioceptive and tactile input. The grip-and-twist motion activates deep pressure receptors in the skin and underlying tissue simultaneously. It can function as sensory seeking (automatic reinforcement), anxiety discharge (the physical sensation interrupts the anxiety loop), pain-masking (counter-stimulation for internal pain), or as a learned escape behaviour when demands are present.
⚠️ Documentation Challenge
Self-pinching bruises are often mistaken for accidental injury or — in India's context — sometimes attributed to rough play or falls. Caregivers and professionals must document every mark: location, size, shape, date, and context. This documentation protects the child, informs the FBA, and ensures medical evaluation when bruising is extensive or unexplained.
Evidence: Level I — FBA + tactile/proprioceptive replacement + anxiety assessment. NCAEP 2020
D-411B · Self-Pinching
Self-Pinching — What You'll Do
The intervention for self-pinching targets the same deep pressure and proprioceptive need — but through safe channels. The hands need something to grip, squeeze, and press. Give them that. The goal is not to stop the hands from seeking input; it is to redirect them to a safe target that delivers equivalent sensation.
Step 1: Hands-Busy Protocol
The hands must never be idle. Fidget tools with grip resistance: therapy putty, stress balls, grip rings, textured squeeze toys. The tool must be in the child's hands BEFORE the pinching begins — proactive placement, not reactive redirection. In India: rubber stress balls, clay, dough, grip trainers are all accessible and affordable.
Step 2: Deep Pressure Alternatives
Provide the same deep pressure the pinching delivers: firm arm squeezes (self-applied or caregiver-applied), compression sleeves on arms, weighted lap pads, wall push-ups, carrying heavy items. The proprioceptive system needs to be fed — schedule this every 60–90 minutes.
Step 3: Protective Clothing
Long sleeves and compression garments during high-risk periods reduce both access to skin and the tactile trigger of exposed skin. This is harm reduction while the replacement behaviour is being established. Not a permanent solution.
Step 4: Anxiety Assessment
When self-pinching is anxiety-driven, the anxiety must be addressed directly (C2 domain). Identify the triggers, reduce demand load, build predictability. The pinching is the symptom; the anxiety is the cause.

Evidence: Level I — FBA + proprioceptive replacement + hands-busy protocol + anxiety intervention. NCAEP 2020
D-412A · Teeth Grinding (Bruxism)
The Sound at Night. The Worn Enamel. The Jaw That Never Rests.
Bruxism — the grinding, clenching, and gnashing of teeth. In children with ASD, bruxism occurs at rates far exceeding the neurotypical population. It happens during sleep, during waking hours, during transitions, during anxiety, and during sensory overload. The sound of teeth grinding is unmistakable. The damage it causes — worn enamel, cracked teeth, jaw pain, headaches, temporomandibular joint dysfunction — accumulates silently over months and years.
The Oral Sensory Drive
Bruxism in ASD is frequently sensory-driven — the jaw muscles provide intense proprioceptive feedback through the temporomandibular joint. The grinding sensation itself may be reinforcing. It can also be anxiety-driven: the jaw clenches as part of the body's stress response, and in children who cannot verbalise distress, the jaw becomes the outlet. Both functions require different interventions — FBA is essential.
⚠️ Dental Consequences
Bruxism causes irreversible enamel erosion, tooth sensitivity, cracked and fractured teeth, gum recession, and TMJ dysfunction causing chronic jaw pain and headaches. Every child with significant bruxism requires dental evaluation. A custom night guard (occlusal splint) may be necessary. In India, paediatric dentists with ASD experience are available in major cities — referral is essential.
Evidence: Level II — Dental evaluation + FBA + oral sensory replacement + anxiety assessment. NCAEP 2020 | Dental literature
D-412B · Teeth Grinding (Bruxism)
Teeth Grinding — What You'll Do
Bruxism intervention works on two tracks simultaneously: protecting the teeth from damage while the behaviour is being addressed, and reducing the underlying sensory or anxiety drive that is fuelling the grinding. Both tracks must run in parallel — dental protection alone does not reduce the behaviour.
Dental Protection First
Dental Protection First — Night guard or occlusal splint fitted by a paediatric dentist. This is non-negotiable for children with significant bruxism. It does not stop the grinding — it protects the teeth while the behaviour plan works. In India, custom-fitted guards are available at most dental colleges and paediatric dental clinics at reasonable cost.
Oral Sensory Replacement
Oral Sensory Replacement — Provide jaw-input alternatives throughout the day: chew tools rated for high-intensity use, crunchy and chewy foods at mealtimes, gum (where appropriate), vibrating oral tools. The jaw needs proprioceptive input — give it a safe, scheduled channel so it is not starved by the time sleep arrives.
Anxiety and Transition Support
Anxiety and Transition Support — When bruxism spikes during transitions, demands, or anxiety, address those triggers directly. Visual schedules, transition warnings, demand fading, and relaxation strategies reduce the anxiety load that drives jaw clenching. Track when grinding is loudest — the pattern reveals the trigger.
Sleep Hygiene
Sleep Hygiene — Bruxism is often worst during sleep. Consistent sleep schedule, reduced screen time before bed, calming bedtime routine, and addressing sleep anxiety (common in ASD) can reduce nocturnal grinding significantly. Melatonin (under medical supervision) may help where sleep onset is disrupted.

D-413A · Ear Hitting / Ear Covering
Hands Over Ears. Fists Against Ears. The Auditory System in Crisis.
Ear hitting — striking the side of the head over the ears with open palm or closed fist. Ear covering — pressing hands hard against ears, sometimes with rocking. These two behaviours share the same target — the auditory system — but have opposite functions. Ear covering is sensory avoidance: the world is too loud. Ear hitting is sensory seeking: the child is generating internal sound and pressure to override external input or to self-stimulate. Distinguishing between them is the first clinical task.
Ear Covering — Avoidance
The auditory environment is overwhelming. Sounds that seem normal to others — fans, traffic, voices, fluorescent lights — are experienced as painful or unbearable. The child covers their ears to reduce input. This is a communication: "This is too loud for me." The intervention is environmental modification and auditory desensitisation — not behaviour reduction. Reducing the aversive input removes the need for the behaviour.
Ear Hitting — Seeking
The child is generating auditory and proprioceptive input by striking the ear. The impact creates internal sound (bone conduction), pressure, and vestibular stimulation simultaneously. This is automatic reinforcement — the hitting itself is rewarding. It may also indicate ear pain: otitis media (ear infection) is extremely common in India's climate and must be ruled out medically before any behaviour plan begins.
Evidence: Level I — Audiological evaluation + FBA (avoidance vs. seeking) + environmental modification or sensory replacement. NCAEP 2020
D-413B · Ear Hitting / Ear Covering
Ear Hitting / Ear Covering — What You'll Do
The intervention pathway splits immediately based on function. Ear covering (avoidance) and ear hitting (seeking) require completely different responses. Applying the wrong intervention — for example, trying to reduce ear covering through extinction — will increase distress and worsen the behaviour. Function identification is not optional here.
Medical Evaluation
Rule out otitis media (ear infection), fluid in the ear, and hearing loss. In India's climate, ear infections are extremely common and frequently missed in non-verbal children. New-onset ear hitting or sudden increase in ear covering requires ENT evaluation before any behaviour plan is developed.
For Ear Covering
Reduce the auditory load. Noise-cancelling headphones or ear defenders during high-noise environments (markets, weddings, school assemblies). Identify and eliminate unnecessary noise sources at home. Provide advance warning before loud sounds. Gradually build auditory tolerance through systematic desensitisation — never force exposure.
For Ear Hitting
Provide alternative auditory and proprioceptive input: music through headphones (the child controls the input), vibrating tools held against the jaw or skull, rhythmic drumming on a drum pad, deep pressure to the head and ears through firm hand compression. Schedule these proactively.
Functional Communication
Teach the child to signal "too loud" or "I need sound" using AAC, PECS, or a simple gesture. When the child can communicate the need, the SIB becomes unnecessary. This is the long-term goal for both functions.

D-414A · Anxiety-Driven SIB
The SIB Is Not the Problem. The Anxiety Is the Problem.
Anxiety-driven SIB is the most common and most mismanaged category in clinical practice. The behaviour plan targets the SIB. The SIB reduces temporarily. Then it returns — because the anxiety was never addressed. This is the cycle that exhausts families and frustrates professionals. Anxiety-driven SIB requires a dual-track intervention: the SIB behaviour plan AND a comprehensive anxiety intervention running simultaneously. Treating only one track will fail.
How to Identify Anxiety-Driven SIB
The SIB occurs predictably before or during specific situations: transitions, demands, unexpected changes, social situations, waiting, loud environments. The child's body shows anxiety signs before the SIB begins: increased heart rate, flushing, rocking, hand-wringing, vocal changes. The SIB provides relief — it discharges the anxiety through physical sensation. After the SIB, the child appears calmer. This relief is the reinforcer.
The Anxiety-SIB Cycle
Trigger → Anxiety builds → SIB occurs → Anxiety discharges → Temporary relief → Trigger returns → Cycle repeats. Each cycle strengthens the SIB as an anxiety management strategy. The child is not being manipulative — they have found the most effective tool available to them for managing an overwhelming internal state. The intervention must provide a better tool, not just remove this one.
Evidence: Level I — Anxiety assessment + FBA + dual-track intervention (SIB plan + anxiety intervention). NCAEP 2020 | C2 domain link
D-414B · Anxiety-Driven SIB
Anxiety-Driven SIB — What You'll Do
The intervention for anxiety-driven SIB is not a behaviour plan. It is a behaviour plan PLUS an anxiety intervention. The behaviour plan manages the SIB in the moment. The anxiety intervention removes the fuel that drives it. Both must be designed and implemented simultaneously by the BCBA in collaboration with the family.
Antecedent Modification
Identify every trigger through ABC data collection. Then systematically reduce the trigger load: visual schedules to reduce unpredictability, transition warnings (5-minute, 2-minute, 1-minute), demand fading during high-anxiety periods, environmental modifications to reduce sensory triggers. Remove the anxiety before it builds to SIB threshold.
Anxiety Discharge Alternatives
The child needs a physical way to discharge anxiety that is safer than SIB. Options: deep pressure (bear hug, compression vest), vigorous movement (running, jumping, crash pad), breathing tools (pinwheel, bubble blowing), cold water on wrists, ice pack on neck. These must be taught and practised during calm periods — not introduced for the first time during crisis.
Functional Communication Training
The child must be able to communicate "I'm overwhelmed" or "I need a break" before the anxiety reaches SIB threshold. AAC device, PECS card, simple gesture, or a visual "help" card. When the child can signal distress, the SIB becomes unnecessary. FCT is the single most powerful long-term intervention for anxiety-driven SIB.
Systematic Desensitisation
Gradually and systematically expose the child to anxiety triggers at sub-threshold intensity, pairing each exposure with preferred items and calm support. Build tolerance over weeks and months. This is the anxiety intervention track — it must run alongside the SIB behaviour plan, not after it.

D-415A · SIB Crisis Protocol
The SIB Has Escalated. This Is a Crisis. Here Is What You Do.
A SIB crisis is when the behaviour has escalated beyond the child's ability to self-regulate and beyond the caregiver's ability to redirect. The child is in a full escalation cycle: the SIB is intense, frequent, and not responding to usual strategies. Injury is occurring or imminent. Every family and every professional working with a child who has SIB must have a written crisis protocol — agreed upon in advance, practised, and accessible. A crisis is not the time to improvise.
What NOT to Do in a Crisis
Do NOT restrain — physical restraint increases distress, escalates the crisis, and can cause injury to both child and caregiver. Do NOT shout, threaten, or demand compliance — the child's cortisol is already maximal. Do NOT introduce new demands or consequences. Do NOT crowd the child — multiple people approaching increases overwhelm. Do NOT try to reason or explain — the prefrontal cortex is offline during crisis. Every one of these responses will make the crisis worse.
The Crisis Escalation Curve
Every SIB crisis follows a predictable curve: trigger → anxiety build-up → escalation → peak → de-escalation → recovery. The most effective intervention happens at the EARLIEST stage — during build-up, before peak. At peak, the only goal is safety. After peak, during recovery, the child needs quiet, low-demand time — not processing, not consequences, not discussion. Recovery takes 20–45 minutes minimum.
Evidence: Level I — Written crisis protocol mandatory for all children with SIB. Safety planning + de-escalation training for all caregivers. NCAEP 2020

D-415B · SIB Crisis Protocol
SIB Crisis Protocol — What You'll Do
The crisis protocol is a written document, not a mental checklist. It is created during a calm period, agreed upon by the BCBA, family, and school team, and placed in every environment where the child spends time. Every caregiver who works with the child must be trained on it. The protocol has three phases: prevention, active crisis response, and post-crisis recovery.
01
Phase 1 — Prevention (Before Crisis)
Know the child's escalation signals. Create a written list of early warning signs specific to this child. When early signs appear: reduce demands immediately, offer preferred items, move to a quieter space, provide sensory input proactively. The goal is to interrupt the escalation curve before it reaches peak. Prevention is always more effective than crisis management.
02
Phase 2 — Active Crisis (At Peak)
Ensure physical safety only. Clear the environment of hard surfaces and dangerous objects. Maintain a calm, quiet presence — no talking, no demands, no eye contact pressure. Use minimal physical guidance only if injury is imminent (hand between head and surface, never full restraint). One person manages the child; others leave the space. Wait. The peak will pass.
03
Phase 3 — Post-Crisis Recovery
The child needs 20–45 minutes of quiet, low-demand time after peak. No processing, no consequences, no discussion of the behaviour. Offer water, a preferred item, a quiet space. The child's nervous system needs to return to baseline. Attempting to teach or correct during recovery will trigger re-escalation.
04
Phase 4 — Post-Crisis Review
After the child has fully recovered (hours later or the next day), the team reviews: What triggered this? What early signs were missed? What worked? What didn't? Update the crisis protocol. Every crisis is data. Use it to prevent the next one.
